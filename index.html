What Satyam has created

Satyam has mapped a business operating model, not a computer OS. It is a driving manual for how one customer request should move through Wiom without teams inventing their own shortcuts.

01

The customer request is the car. The business journey is the road. The OS document explains how that car is supposed to move from start to finish.

02

Each OS is a checkpoint with one job. Coverage checks if the location can be served. Allocation chooses who serves the customer. Lifecycle tracks where the customer is in the journey.

03

Primitives are the road laws: truths that must always hold. Guards are the gates and barriers that block the car when someone tries to break those truths.

04

Parameters are the numbers on the signs: radius, wait time, cap, threshold, retry count. You can tune them without rebuilding the whole road.

05

The state machine says which bay the car is in right now and which move is allowed next. That stops random jumps, duplicate work, and unclear ownership.

06

The point of the whole map is simple: keep the customer car on the right road, through the right checkpoints, with clear rules for what is allowed, blocked, and tunable.

Deeper guide

How to read the OS map when the car is real

Use this when someone asks, “what does this OS document actually decide?” The answer should always come back to the customer car, the checkpoint that owns the next decision, and the rules that stop bad shortcuts.

Step 1

Start with one customer request

Do not begin with tables, services, or diagrams. Begin with one customer request: phone number, location, selected plan, source, current status, and what the customer is trying to get done.

The OS map is useful only if it can say where this request is now and what should happen next.

Step 2

Find the checkpoint that owns the next decision

A checkpoint is an owner. Coverage should decide whether the location can be served. Allocation should decide who serves it. Lifecycle should decide the official journey state.

If two teams can make the same decision, the map is weak. If no team clearly owns the decision, the car gets stuck.

Step 3

Read the primitives as non-negotiable laws

Primitives are the truths the system must protect even when a shortcut looks faster. Example: one customer request should have one official state, one official owner for a decision, and one controlled path forward.

If a primitive is violated, the journey may still move, but the business loses trust in the data and the ownership model.

Step 4

Check the guards that block illegal moves

A guard is the enforcement mechanism. It can be a validation, state check, duplicate check, permissions rule, or hard block in the workflow.

The simple test: if someone tries to skip the owner checkpoint, double-assign the customer, or jump to an invalid state, what stops them?

Step 5

Separate tuneable parameters from hard rules

Parameters are knobs, not new logic. Radius, wait time, retry count, partner cap, and thresholds should be visible settings that can be changed deliberately.

This keeps the system flexible without letting people hide business decisions inside random code paths.

Step 6

Use the state machine to prevent confusion

State says where the car is right now. Transitions say where it is allowed to go next. This matters because teams often disagree when the same customer appears to be in two places at once.

A good OS map makes the next allowed move obvious and makes bad jumps impossible or clearly exceptional.

The map should answer these questions

  • Where is the customer request right now?
  • Which checkpoint owns the next decision?
  • What truth must not be broken?
  • What guard blocks a bad shortcut?
  • Which parameter can be tuned without changing the rule?
  • What is the next allowed state?
